Background & Methodology

Key
Concepts

The physical phenomena, governing equations, and computational framework underpinning the perforated impedance‑gradient pad design.

The Problem

The Edge Effect

When current passes between two surface electrodes, streamlines converge at the electrode perimeter, producing localised regions of high current density — “hotspots” — that drive pain and third‑degree burns during prolonged pacing.

  • Current density increases as streamlines compress at the pad rim
  • Third‑degree burns reported after as little as 2 hours of pacing
  • Sedated patients cannot report pain, so injury progresses unseen

Individual Research · Jaehoon

Voltage BC Model

A Dirichlet boundary‑condition formulation of the electrode–torso problem. Electrodes are modelled as equipotential metal pads on the skin, the torso as a conductive bulk, and air as an insulator.

  • Top pad: V = Vapplied (Dirichlet)
  • Bottom pad: V = 0 (ground)
  • Skin exposed to air: ∂V/∂n = 0 (Neumann, no flux)
  • Interface layer: 0.5–1.5 mm thin gel layer with resistivity ρ models the gel/skin contact impedance
  • Perimeter ring (Krasteva 2002): ρcentre = 20 Ωm, ρring = 100 Ωm — raising edge resistivity redistributes current inward

Result: K dropped from 3.12 → 2.71 (≈13% improvement) in the initial validation against Krasteva’s reference geometry, confirming the impedance‑gradient principle before being carried forward into the group design.

Governing Physics

Laplace & Current Density

At low pacing frequencies the tissue behaves as a quasi‑static resistive medium. Charge conservation under Ohm’s law gives the generalised Laplace equation, solved for the scalar potential V(x, z).

−∇·[σ(x,z) ∇V] = 0
|J| = |−σ ∇V|

Solved with the MATLAB PDE Toolbox on a 2D sagittal cross‑section (0.50 × 0.25 m) with a 45 mm cardiac inclusion. σtissue = 0.2 S/m, σheart = 0.5 S/m, σmetal derived from Ag/AgCl surface resistivity ρ = 5 Ω and d = 1 mm.

Performance Metric

Non‑Uniformity Ratio K

K = Jmax / Jmin

Evaluated along a horizontal line 0.5 mm below the electrode–tissue interface, K quantifies how evenly current is injected into the tissue.

  • K = 1 → perfectly uniform delivery
  • K ≫ 1 → strong edge concentration, higher burn risk
  • Paired with peak |J| in tissue as the second objective: directly linked to localised heating

Design Exploration · Group Research

Impedance Profile Strategies

Three impedance‑gradient strategies were evaluated for suppressing pad‑edge hotspots while preserving overall current delivery (K). Each balances manufacturability, edge suppression, and the risk of secondary hotspots forming at internal transitions.

1. Stepped Ring

Discrete high‑resistivity ring at the perimeter. Highly effective at suppressing the classic edge hotspot, but the sharp inner transition can spawn a secondary hotspot, often hurting K.

Simple · Manufacturable

2. Tanh / Logistic Ramp

Smooth sigmoid increase in resistivity near the perimeter. Suppresses edges without a sharp internal boundary; generally yields the best K and is robust to mesh and geometry.

Used in back‑pad sweep

3. Power‑Law Gradient

ρ(r) ∝ rp, with p between 2 and 4 giving an idealised gradient electrode. Low K and smooth profiles, but requires careful tuning of the exponent.

Research baseline

4. Discrete Perforations

Approximates a smooth gradient with manufacturable laser‑cut holes in the Ag/AgCl layer. Carried forward into NSGA‑II optimisation and the final concentric‑ring design.

Final design

A preliminary 3‑band model (low‑ρ centre, intermediate band, high‑ρ edge) confirmed that a graded resistivity profile reduces K from 2.18 → 1.91 (≈17% reduction in Jmax at the edge) without compromising deep myocardial field strength. This validated the impedance‑gradient concept that the final perforated design implements physically.

Final Report · Computational Framework

NSGA‑II Multi‑Objective Optimisation

NSGA‑II (Non‑dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II, Deb et al. 2002) is an elitist evolutionary algorithm built for problems with two or more competing objectives. Instead of returning one “best” design, it returns a Pareto front — the set of designs for which no single objective can be improved without making another worse.

Step 1

Initialise

Random population of 60 candidate designs in the 3‑D variable space.

Step 2

Evaluate

Run the FEM solver to compute (K, peak |J|) for each design.

Step 3

Sort & Rank

Non‑dominated sort by Pareto dominance; assign crowding distances to preserve diversity.

Step 4

Crossover & Mutate

Generate offspring with crossover fraction 0.80; integer constraint on hole count.

Step 5

Repeat

40 generations · ≈ 2,400 FEM evaluations total · Pareto fraction 0.35.

Design Variables

  • Hole radius: 0.2 – 2.5 mm
  • Holes per side: 2 – 30 (integer)
  • rperf: 5 – 45 mm — radius at which perforations begin

Objectives (both minimised)

  • K: current non‑uniformity at skin
  • peak |J| in tissue: localised heating & burn risk

The Pareto front spanned K ≈ 2.33 – 2.60 and peak |J| ≈ 34.6 – 35.4 A/m². Sensitivity analysis showed rperf was the most influential variable (every Pareto‑optimal design had rperf < 10 mm), with hole radius exhibiting a nonlinear threshold above ≈ 1.5 mm.

Translation to Manufacturable Design

Concentric‑Ring Electrode

The NSGA‑II findings were translated into an 8‑ring concentric layout matching the laser cutter’s 0.4 mm minimum feature size, with hole density increasing from centre to edge to concentrate impedance where the edge effect is strongest.
