Background & Methodology

Key
Concepts

The physical phenomena, governing equations, and computational framework underpinning the perforated impedance-gradient pad design.

The Problem

The Edge Effect

When current passes between two surface electrodes, streamlines converge at the electrode perimeter, producing localised regions of high current density — "hotspots" — that drive pain and third-degree burns during prolonged pacing.

  • Current density increases as streamlines compress at the pad rim
  • Third-degree burns reported after as little as 2 hours of pacing
  • Sedated patients cannot report pain, so injury progresses unseen

Individual Research · Jaehoon

Voltage BC Model

A Dirichlet boundary-condition formulation of the electrode–torso problem. Electrodes are modelled as equipotential metal pads on the skin, the torso as a conductive bulk, and air as an insulator.

  • Top pad: V = Vapplied (Dirichlet)
  • Bottom pad: V = 0 (ground)
  • Skin exposed to air: ∂V/∂n = 0 (Neumann, no flux)
  • Perimeter ring (Krasteva 2002): ρcentre = 20 Ωm, ρring = 100 Ωm

Result: K dropped from 3.12 → 2.71 (≈13% improvement), confirming the impedance-gradient principle before the group design.

Governing Physics

Laplace & Current Density

At low pacing frequencies the tissue behaves as a quasi-static resistive medium. Charge conservation under Ohm's law gives the generalised Laplace equation, solved for the scalar potential V(x, z).

−∇·[σ(x,z) ∇V] = 0
|J| = |−σ ∇V|

Solved with the MATLAB PDE Toolbox on a 2D sagittal cross-section (0.50 × 0.25 m). σtissue = 0.2 S/m, σheart = 0.5 S/m.

Performance Metric

Non-Uniformity Ratio K

K = Jmax / Jmin

Evaluated along a horizontal line 0.5 mm below the electrode–tissue interface.

  • K = 1 → perfectly uniform delivery
  • K ≫ 1 → strong edge concentration, higher burn risk
  • Paired with peak |J| as the second optimisation objective

Design Exploration · Group Research

Impedance Profile Strategies

Three impedance-gradient strategies were evaluated for suppressing pad-edge hotspots while preserving overall current delivery.

1. Stepped Ring

Discrete high-resistivity ring at the perimeter. Effective at edge suppression but can create secondary hotspots at the inner boundary.

Simple · Manufacturable

2. Tanh / Logistic Ramp

Smooth sigmoid increase in resistivity near the perimeter. Best K improvement, robust to mesh and geometry variations.

Used in back-pad sweep

3. Power-Law Gradient

ρ(r) ∝ rp, p between 2 and 4. Low K and smooth profiles but requires careful exponent tuning.

Research baseline

4. Discrete Perforations

Approximates a smooth gradient with laser-cut holes. Carried into NSGA-II optimisation and the final concentric-ring design.

Final design

Final Report · Computational Framework

NSGA-II Multi-Objective Optimisation

NSGA-II (Deb et al. 2002) returns a Pareto front — designs for which no objective can be improved without worsening another.

Step 1

Initialise

Random population of 60 candidate designs.

Step 2

Evaluate

Run FEM to compute (K, peak |J|) for each design.

Step 3

Sort & Rank

Non-dominated sort; crowding distance for diversity.

Step 4

Crossover

Crossover fraction 0.80; integer constraint on hole count.

Step 5

Repeat

40 generations · ≈2,400 FEM evaluations · Pareto fraction 0.35.

Design Variables

  • Hole radius: 0.2 – 2.5 mm
  • Holes per side: 2 – 30 (integer)
  • rperf: 5 – 45 mm

Objectives (minimised)

  • K: current non-uniformity at skin
  • peak |J|: localised heating & burn risk
